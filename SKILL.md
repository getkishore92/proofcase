# Proofcase

Build evidence for a hiring decision. Preserve the user's voice, actual contribution, and confidentiality constraints.

## Operating principles

1. Start from evidence. Inspect every artifact the user provides before proposing a story.
2. Tailor the proof to the target role, company, domain, and seniority.
3. Make ownership explicit. Separate the user's work from team work.
4. Organize around decisions, constraints, and outcomes. Remove generic design-process scaffolding.
5. Support scanning and deep reading. Put the strongest proof near the top, then supply enough detail to defend it in an interview.
6. Show final work near the top. Use process material when it explains a consequential decision.
7. Treat missing evidence as missing. Do not invent research, metrics, quotes, causality, dates, tools, or shipped status.
8. Show counterevidence. Include failed bets, negative or flat results, limits, and the team's response when they affected the work.
9. Keep AI use factual. Explain what the user did with AI, what they verified, and where human judgment changed the result.
10. Protect confidential work. Offer redaction, ranges, indexing, reconstructed diagrams, or password-protected delivery.
11. State limits. A case study can improve fit and interview readiness; it cannot guarantee a job.

## Choose the mode

Infer the mode from the request. Confirm when the choice changes the deliverable.

- **Audit:** Diagnose an existing case study and rank fixes.
- **Extract:** Interview the user and reconstruct a case study from incomplete material.
- **Rewrite:** Preserve facts while improving structure, copy, and visual direction.
- **Tailor:** Adapt a strong base case study to a job description without changing facts.
- **Build:** Create the full case study from raw artifacts.
- **Present:** Convert the story into an interview deck or spoken walkthrough.

## Phase 1: inspect before asking

Read the case study, job description, portfolio, screenshots, deck, repository, analytics, research, or notes available in the conversation and workspace. When given a live URL or current job listing, inspect it with suitable browser or web tools.

Create a private working inventory:

- confirmed facts and direct evidence
- negative, flat, or mixed results
- claims that need qualification
- missing information that would change the story
- confidentiality limits
- likely target role and seniority
- strongest visuals and weakest visual gaps
- real prototypes, motion, code, demos, or interactive proof

Do not ask the user for information already present in the artifacts.

## Phase 2: run the minimum intake

Read [references/intake-and-seniority.md](references/intake-and-seniority.md). Ask questions when the answers affect the narrative, claims, or output.

Ask no more than five short questions in one round. Prefer one round. Useful questions cover:

1. target role or job description
2. actual role, team, dates, and level of ownership
3. project goal, constraints, and stakes
4. two or three decisions the user influenced
5. shipped result, evidence of change, and sharing limits

For a quick audit, state assumptions and list missing proof. For a publishable case study, pause when the artifacts cannot support a material fact.

## Phase 3: calibrate the hiring signal

Choose a primary profile from [references/intake-and-seniority.md](references/intake-and-seniority.md). Years of experience are one input. Use demonstrated scope, autonomy, complexity, influence, and accountability.

For every target role, identify:

- the hiring question this case study must answer
- three to five capabilities the role requires
- direct proof available for each capability
- proof gaps that copy cannot repair

Do not inflate a project to imitate a higher level. Frame the real scope well.

## Phase 4: build an evidence ledger

Read [references/narrative-and-evidence.md](references/narrative-and-evidence.md). Record each important claim with:

- claim
- source or artifact
- confidence: confirmed, attributed, estimated, or unknown
- ownership: user, team, partner, or company
- safe public wording
- counterevidence, limitation, or cost

Apply these rules:

- Use causal language when the evidence supports causality.
- Label estimates, directional results, and proxy measures.
- Attribute team outcomes while stating the user's contribution.
- Replace unsupported metrics with observable change, adoption evidence, decisions enabled, risk reduced, or a clear learning.
- Never convert correlation into credit.
- Report the baseline, time window, segment, sample, and companion changes when known.
- Do not present a positive number without material tradeoffs, regressions, or uncertainty that affected the decision.

## Phase 5: find the story

Write the project in one sentence:

> I helped [specific people or business] move from [credible before state] to [credible after state] by [the user's distinctive contribution], under [defining constraint].

Then choose one narrative spine:

- a hard decision under constraint
- a before-and-after product change
- a sequence of bets and evidence
- an ambiguous zero-to-one problem
- a system that unlocked repeated delivery
- a failure, correction, and result
- an engineering tradeoff that changed product behavior
- organizational influence that changed direction or execution

Reject any spine that needs invented drama. Avoid the repeated school-project sequence of problem, research, persona, journey map, wireframes, testing, and final UI unless that sequence explains the real work.

## Phase 6: design the case study

Use a two-layer structure.

### Scan layer

The opening should let a recruiter or hiring manager answer these questions at a glance:

- What changed?
- What did this person own?
- How complex or important was the work?
- What proof can I see?
- Is this relevant to my role?

Include a specific title, one-sentence summary, role and scope, timeframe, team, outcome or current status, and one strong visual.

### Deep layer

Use the sections the story earns:

- context and stakes
- role, team, and boundaries
- constraints
- pivotal decisions and rejected options
- research or technical evidence tied to decisions
- selected iterations with reasons
- shipped work or final system
- outcome and confidence level
- reflection grounded in what the user would change

Lead each section with the point. Use visuals as evidence. Caption every visual with what it proves or why it changed.

For detailed patterns, read [references/narrative-and-evidence.md](references/narrative-and-evidence.md). For deliverable shapes, read [references/output-templates.md](references/output-templates.md).

Read [references/industry-patterns.md](references/industry-patterns.md) to choose evidence for B2B, B2C, engineering, design-engineering, motion, prototypes, and interactive presentation.

For transformations or worked explanations, read [references/examples.md](references/examples.md). Use named portfolio examples with permission or a compatible licence. Keep excerpts short, link to the source, and explain the edit. Public access does not grant reproduction rights.

## Phase 7: show AI-era capability with provenance

Add relevant, factual AI content. Useful evidence includes:

- a workflow the user accelerated and the time or iteration gained
- a prototype, code path, analysis, or artifact the user produced with AI
- the checks used to catch errors, bias, hallucinations, unsafe output, or poor UX
- a decision where the user rejected or changed AI output
- a shipped AI feature with evaluation, failure states, human control, and monitoring

Avoid tool lists and claims such as "used AI throughout the process." Name the task, judgment, validation, and result. Show traceable work when public links, code, demos, or artifacts are safe to share.

Exclude manufactured UX process. Keep card sorts, personas, journey maps, affinity maps, or interview counts when they are real and changed a decision. Label synthetic participants, simulated data, concept work, and self-initiated redesigns.

## Phase 8: edit the copy

Write in the user's natural vocabulary. Prefer concrete nouns, active verbs, short paragraphs, and precise attribution.

Run this final copy pass, adapted from Hardik Pandya's MIT-licensed [stop-slop](https://github.com/hardikpandya/stop-slop):

- remove throat-clearing, filler, and inflated claims
- remove formulaic binary contrasts and dramatic fragments
- name the person who acted
- replace vague abstractions with the decision, constraint, or result
- vary sentence length and paragraph endings
- remove repeated conclusions and generic lessons
- cut any sentence that could describe another designer's project unchanged

Credit stop-slop when publishing a derivative checklist or bundled copy guidance. Do not claim its rules as original work.

## Phase 9: audit and revise

Read [references/rubric.md](references/rubric.md). Score the draft before delivery. Fix critical failures first:

1. unclear ownership
2. unsupported or misleading claims
3. weak role fit
4. buried result or final work
5. generic process theater
6. missing decision rationale
7. poor visual evidence
8. long or synthetic-sounding copy
9. a success-only story with no limits, tradeoffs, or learning
10. low-value process artifacts presented as proof

Do not chase a perfect score by adding length. Explain proof gaps the user must fill.

## Deliver the result

Match the user's request. A full engagement should produce:

1. **Positioning:** target role, seniority signal, and one-sentence case-study promise
2. **Evidence gaps:** missing proof and ways to recover or represent it without invention
3. **Revised case study:** publishable copy with visual placeholders and captions
4. **Change log:** the most consequential structural and copy changes
5. **Interview defense:** likely questions, weak points, and concise answers grounded in the record
6. **Optional variants:** recruiter skim, full web case study, presentation outline, and job-tailored version

When useful, add one or two focused before-and-after excerpts. Label any reconstructed example and preserve the facts in both versions.

When editing files, preserve the original unless the user authorizes an in-place change. For a review, do not publish, deploy, or change external systems.

## Research basis

Read [references/research.md](references/research.md) when explaining current hiring context, comparing portfolio conventions, or updating the skill's guidance. Treat time-sensitive hiring claims as research that needs a current check.
