# Junior and low-evidence case studies

Use this reference for emerging or junior candidates, first case studies, internships, coursework, self-initiated projects, unshipped work, NDA-limited work, or projects without product analytics.

## Coach the candidate

- Review one section or claim at a time.
- Explain the hiring question behind each suggested change.
- Ask one or two questions, then revise from the answer.
- Preserve the candidate's vocabulary and level of responsibility.
- End each round with the next two actions.
- Do not demand senior strategy, company-wide influence, or large metrics.

## Evidence ladder

Prefer the strongest evidence the project can support:

1. **Shipped or testable work:** a live product, prototype, code path, research artifact, or implementation the reviewer can inspect.
2. **Decision trace:** an iteration, rejected option, critique, technical constraint, or finding that changed the work.
3. **Observed response:** usability behavior, quality checks, support patterns, adoption, delivery change, or another bounded observation.
4. **Attributed feedback:** a named role or documented source describing how the work affected a decision.
5. **Grounded reflection:** a specific mistake, correction, or lesson tied to an artifact or decision.

Use lower-rung evidence without presenting it as a business outcome. A case study may combine several rungs.

## Common project situations

### Internship or team project

- State the assignment, team boundaries, supervision, and decisions the candidate influenced.
- Credit mentors and partners by role.
- Show how the candidate responded to critique or implementation constraints.
- Replace broad claims such as "led the redesign" with the exact flow, artifact, or decision owned.

### Coursework or self-initiated project

- Label the work as coursework, concept work, or self-initiated work near the opening.
- State how the candidate chose the problem and found participants or source material.
- Separate real research from simulated data, synthetic participants, assumptions, and desk research.
- Score the reasoning, craft, and validation that exist. Do not imply launch or adoption.

### Unshipped work

- Label the status as concept, prototype, pilot, paused, cancelled, or unshipped.
- Use the decision enabled, risk exposed, test result, or technical learning as the outcome.
- Explain why the work stopped when the reason is known and safe to share.

### NDA-limited work

- Preserve the type of user, constraint, decision, and contribution while removing protected names and values.
- Use indexed metrics, ranges, redacted screens, or reconstructed diagrams only when the reconstruction remains accurate.
- Label every reconstruction and remove confidential source material from the public deliverable.

### No product analytics

- Look for observable changes in behavior, workflow, delivery, quality, support, accessibility, reliability, or decision confidence.
- Record the measurement gap and the test the team would run next.
- Do not convert stakeholder praise, prototype feedback, or completion into product impact.

### AI-assisted work

- Name the artifact or task the candidate used AI to produce.
- Show what the candidate checked, rejected, or changed.
- Keep generated research, synthetic participants, and simulated results labeled.

## Junior review output

Return:

1. the likely recruiter takeaway
2. the strongest evidence already present
3. the largest credibility or clarity gap
4. an explanation of the hiring question behind that gap
5. one or two questions for the candidate
6. a revised excerpt after the candidate answers
7. the next two actions

Keep the full rubric available, but do not lead with a long score breakdown unless the user asks for an audit.
