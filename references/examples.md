# Before and after examples

These examples come from Kishore Sundarajan's public portfolio and are included with the skill author's permission. The revisions improve presentation of existing work. They do not claim that copy changes caused the product outcomes.

## 1. Pocket FM: move impact forward

Source: [Pocket FM monetization case study](https://kishore.design/work/pocket-fm-monetization)

### Before

The case-study navigation placed **Impact** after the project timeline. A long timeline entry mixed launch scope, personal contribution, category novelty, and a trial-adoption metric:

> Shipped the first end-to-end subscription experience across India and USA. I built the daily unlock construct, onboarding flow, and paywall surfaces from scratch. Trial adoption hit ~55% on allocation, strong for a product category that did not exist six weeks earlier.

### After

The case study moved **Impact** directly after the summary. The timeline kept only the event and evidence:

> Launched the first end-to-end subscription experience across India and USA; trial adoption reached ~55% on allocation.

The new impact section gave the larger result a dedicated visual hierarchy, including subscription share of revenue, active subscribers, trial adoption, listening depth, resubscription, and conversion at a key touchpoint.

### Why it improved

- A reviewer can find proof before reading the full process.
- The timeline now does one job.
- The result and the user's decisions remain separate, which makes attribution easier to defend.
- The case study supports a fast scan without removing depth.

### Rule to reuse

Move the strongest defensible result near the top. Keep chronology only when timing explains a decision or change.

## 2. Pocket FM: replace assertive shorthand with evidence

Source: [Pocket FM monetization case study](https://kishore.design/work/pocket-fm-monetization)

### Before

> After V1, product wanted savings messaging everywhere. I pushed back. Users who understood the offer did not need more prompts. They needed a better product. For V2, I cut nudge frequency and let daily unlock usage do the converting.

### After

> I limited Premium nudges to payment and unlock moments. The initial V2 direction added more savings prompts, but V1 data showed reminders were not the bottleneck after users understood the offer. I shifted the work toward clearer value, trust, and daily unlock usage.

### Why it improved

- It names the changed behavior: fewer nudges and where they remained.
- It replaces dramatic fragments with the evidence used to decide.
- It avoids casting a partner team as the obstacle.
- It gives the user a stronger interview path: signal, choice, and tradeoff.

### Rule to reuse

Replace "I pushed back" stories with the disagreement, evidence, decision, and shipped behavior.

## 3. Togai: clarify ownership and outcome

Source: [Togai usage-pricing case study](https://kishore.design/work/togai-usage-pricing)

### Before

> **Solution:** Usage ingestion, pricing configuration, invoicing, and real-time insights, designed from scratch as one connected system.

> **Outcome:** Designed the product from scratch. Shipped MVP and v1.5. Acquired by Zuora.

### After

> **Solution:** I designed Togai from scratch across usage ingestion, pricing setup, invoicing, and real-time insights, then shaped v1.5 into a reusable system.

> **Outcome:** The team shipped MVP and v1.5, demos became easier, components scaled across the product, and Zuora acquired Togai.

### Why it improved

- The solution states the user's contribution directly.
- The outcome credits the team for shipping.
- The acquisition stays company context instead of becoming a personal causal claim.
- The intermediate effects explain how the design work helped before the acquisition.

### Rule to reuse

Use "I" for the candidate's work, "we" or "the team" for shared delivery, and the company name for company outcomes.

## 4. Togai: replace placeholders with product evidence

Source: [Togai usage-pricing case study](https://kishore.design/work/togai-usage-pricing)

### Before

The page contained generic prompts such as:

> Use the screen where teams define the plan or pricing rule.

> Use a comparison that shows how the flow became easier to understand.

### After

The page used real MVP and v2 screens with captions tied to decisions:

> Teams entered plan name, billing cycle, currencies, grace period, and trial rules on one long setup screen.

> I split billing details into a tighter step and kept the preview visible beside it.

### Why it improved

- The visuals prove that the work existed.
- The captions direct attention to the product change.
- The before-and-after pair exposes a concrete design decision.
- A reviewer can assess craft and reasoning without reading a long process account.

### Rule to reuse

Pair each major claim with an artifact and a caption that states what changed.
