# Intake and seniority calibration

Use this reference to choose questions and expected proof. Do not assign level from years alone.

## Intake decision tree

Ask for the target job description when the user wants conversion or tailoring. Ask for the base case study and raw artifacts when the user wants a rewrite. Ask for a spoken account when documents are thin.

Prioritize missing facts in this order:

1. ownership and team boundaries
2. shipped status and outcome
3. target role
4. pivotal decisions and constraints
5. visuals, research, code, or data that prove the account
6. confidentiality limits

Good follow-up prompts:

- "Which part would not have happened in the same way without you?"
- "What changed between the first plausible direction and the shipped one?"
- "Who disagreed, and what evidence resolved it?"
- "What did engineering, product, research, or design own?"
- "What result can you defend in an interview?"
- "Which artifact best proves that decision?"

Avoid asking for a generic end-to-end process. Ask about the project-specific work.

## Level signals

### Emerging or junior

Emphasize:

- sound fundamentals and attention to detail
- ability to follow evidence and explain choices
- response to critique
- collaboration and reliable execution
- learning from a real constraint or mistake

Do not demand company-level strategy or large metrics. A small shipped improvement can carry the story when the reasoning and craft are visible.

### Mid-level

Emphasize:

- ownership of a defined problem or flow
- independent decisions within a team
- prioritization and tradeoffs
- collaboration through implementation
- evidence that the work changed product behavior or delivery

Show where the user moved beyond assigned screens.

### Senior individual contributor

Emphasize:

- ownership through ambiguity
- connection between user, business, and technical constraints
- quality of high-consequence decisions
- cross-functional influence
- shipped outcomes and follow-through
- ability to reduce scope while protecting value

Show judgment, not a longer process.

### Staff or principal individual contributor

Emphasize:

- direction across products, teams, or a strategic program
- systems, standards, or mechanisms that outlast one launch
- influence without formal authority
- sequencing across short-term delivery and long-term direction
- leverage created for other teams
- second-order effects and organizational learning

Distinguish hands-on contribution from broad influence. One feature story rarely proves staff scope without a wider system or organizational effect.

### Lead or manager

Emphasize:

- team direction, decision quality, and operating conditions
- coaching, hiring, allocation, critique, and standards
- cross-functional alignment and executive communication
- outcomes achieved through the team
- where the manager contributed directly and where they enabled others

Credit reports and partners by role. Do not present team output as solo work.

## Discipline signals

### Product designer

Show product judgment, interaction and visual craft, research use, business context, implementation partnership, and outcome.

### UX researcher

Show research question, method choice, recruitment limits, analysis rigor, confidence, influence on decisions, and what changed.

### Software developer

Show architecture, constraints, alternatives, performance, reliability, security, testing, maintainability, delivery, and product effect. Include code or diagrams only when they prove a decision.

### Design engineer

Show the loop between design and implementation, prototype fidelity, component or system decisions, production constraints, quality details, and shipped behavior.

### Product manager

Show problem selection, prioritization, customer and market evidence, team alignment, tradeoffs, rollout, and business or product result.

## Calibration output

State:

- target profile
- evidence supporting that profile
- signals missing from the project
- claims to avoid
- the strongest honest framing
